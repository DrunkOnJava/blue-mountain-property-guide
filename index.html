I. GOVERNANCE & STRUCTURE

Example of quality construction that meets BMPOA architectural standards and enhances the community's mountain character

A. BMPOA OVERVIEW

The Blue Mountain Property Owners Association (BMPOA) is a Virginia Sanitary District serving the Blue Mountain community in Warren County. Our organization combines the authority of a special taxing district with the community focus of a property owners association.

About BMPOA

Created in the late 1950's as a vacation community for Washington D.C. residents, Blue Mountain is now a mix of summer residents and year-round families. The subdivision operates as a Sanitary District in Warren County, managed by an elected volunteer executive board.

All Board members are Blue Mountain property owners elected at the Annual Meeting for two-year terms.

BOARD ELECTIONS 2025

The Board nomination window is currently open and closes at midnight, June 30th. All nine positions on the Board as well as two positions on the nominating committee are up for election at the annual meeting on August 17, 2025. Any property owner may run for any position on the Board.

B. SANITARY DISTRICT EXPLAINED

1. WHAT IS A SANITARY DISTRICT?

A Sanitary District is a special taxing district allowed by the Code of Virginia. It provides a mechanism for communities like ours to fund necessary infrastructure and services through a dedicated tax assessment on properties within the district.

Key Benefit

Our Sanitary District status ensures that all property owners, including developers, contribute fairly to the maintenance of our roads and community assets.

2. BLUE MOUNTAIN SANITARY DISTRICT

The Blue Mountain Subdivision Sanitary District was created by Order of the Circuit Court of Warren County. By agreement with the Warren County Board of Supervisors, the BMPOA Executive Board serves as the managing agent of the Sanitary District.

3. HOW A SANITARY DISTRICT IS CREATED

Sanitary Districts are established through a formal legal process involving petition to the Circuit Court, public hearings, and court approval. This ensures proper oversight and community input in the creation process.

C. BOARD & COMMITTEES STRUCTURE

EXECUTIVE BOARD

The BMPOA Executive Board consists of nine elected property owners who serve two-year terms. Board members volunteer their time to manage community affairs and represent the interests of all property owners.

Board Responsibilities:

  • Financial oversight and budget management
  • Road maintenance and snow removal planning
  • Community facility management (Lodge, Deer Lake)
  • Policy development and enforcement
  • Long-term planning and capital improvements

COMMITTEE STRUCTURE

Standing Committees:

  • Nominating Committee: Oversees board election process
  • Finance Committee: Budget planning and financial review
  • Roads Committee: Maintenance and improvement planning
  • Lake Committee: Deer Lake management and policies
  • Lodge Committee: Lodge operations and rental management

Committees meet as needed and report to the full Board. Property owners are encouraged to participate in committee work.

D. MEMBERSHIP & MEETINGS

ANNUAL MEETING

The Annual Meeting is held each August and is the primary venue for community-wide decisions. All property owners are members of BMPOA and are encouraged to attend.

Annual Meeting Activities:

  • Board elections
  • Budget presentation and approval
  • Community updates and reports
  • Policy discussions and voting
  • Open forum for community input

BOARD MEETINGS

Regular Board meetings are held monthly and are open to all property owners. Meeting schedules and agendas are posted on the community website and distributed via email.

Meeting Information:

  • Frequency: Monthly (typically second Saturday)
  • Location: The Lodge or announced alternate location
  • Time: 10:00 AM
  • Public Comment: Time allocated for property owner input

SPECIAL MEETINGS

Special meetings may be called as needed to address urgent community matters. Notice requirements and procedures are outlined in the BMPOA bylaws.

E. NEW HOME CONSTRUCTION REQUIREMENTS

OVERVIEW

All new construction and major renovations in Blue Mountain must comply with established architectural and community standards. These requirements help preserve property values and maintain the character of our mountain community.

APPROVAL PROCESS

Required Steps:

  1. Initial Consultation: Contact the Board before beginning design
  2. Plan Submission: Submit architectural plans and site analysis
  3. Board Review: Plans reviewed at next scheduled Board meeting
  4. Approval Letter: Written approval required before construction
  5. Inspection: Final inspection upon completion

DESIGN STANDARDS

Architectural Requirements:

  • Mountain-appropriate design and materials
  • Minimum setback requirements from roads and property lines
  • Height restrictions to preserve views
  • Natural landscaping and tree preservation
  • Driveway and access road specifications

Environmental Considerations:

  • Steep slope building restrictions
  • Stormwater management requirements
  • Well and septic system placement
  • Fire safety and defensible space requirements

Important Note

Construction approval from BMPOA does not replace the need for Warren County building permits and inspections. Property owners must comply with all county requirements in addition to BMPOA standards.

DETAILED CONSTRUCTION REQUIREMENTS

Preamble (September 2024 Update)

Works in conjunction with Blue Mountain Declaration of Protective Covenants, Conditions & Restrictions and BMPOA Bylaws (available on BMPOA.org under "Homeowners")

All submissions/questions go to ARC Chair via "Contact Us" on BMPOA "Homeowners" page; include Name, Email, Property Address; contractors must include sponsoring owner

PRE-CONSTRUCTION REQUIREMENTS

Notification Process

Before applying for any Warren County building permit or zoning variance, owner must notify ARC Chair (include contractors/agents). Submit in writing via return-receipt mail. ARC Chair (or designee) will respond in writing within 30 days with:

  1. Approval
  2. Disapproval (with reasons)
  3. Request for additional information (owner then has 30 days to supply; ARC has 30 days to respond). If no response in 30 days, deemed approved.

Plan Approval

Construction may not begin until building site plan and detailed plans (materials list, tree removal plan) are approved in writing by ARC. Any deviation from approved plans risks legal action.

Construction must be sited unobtrusively—use natural flora to screen from access roads. Only remove trees needed for foundation, septic field, and driveway unless extra clearing is approved. All exterior work (painting, siding) completed within one year of start.

BUILDING REQUIREMENTS

Allowed Structures

  • One detached single-family dwelling per lot
  • One private garage per lot
  • One accessory building per lot

Exterior Materials

  • Wood or wood-look siding (cedar lap, HardiPlank, solid-surface synthetics)
  • No vinyl siding
  • Solid log homes acceptable
  • ARC may grant exceptions case-by-case

Color Requirements

  • Muted, nature-reflective colors (greens, browns, tans)
  • No bright or vibrant colors

Fire Safety

  • Fire-resistant materials strongly advised for exterior (roof, siding, decking)

SETBACK REQUIREMENTS

Road Setbacks

  • ≥ 75 ft from centerline of Route 638
  • ≥ 70 ft from centerline of any internal BMPOA road/right-of-way

Property Line Setbacks

  • ≥ 20 ft from each side line
  • ≥ 25 ft from rear line of any lot
  • ARC/Board may grant exceptions to side/rear setbacks

Size Requirements

  • Minimum dwelling size: 1,000 sq ft living space
  • Manufactured homes must meet Class A design criteria
  • Garage/accessory building must match primary structure's design and materials

ENVIRONMENTAL REQUIREMENTS

Tree and Vegetation Management

  • Trees felled during construction must be staged unobtrusively or removed
  • ARC may allow limited scenic view clearing but not extensive clearing
  • No permanent outside outhouses allowed

BUILDING ON UNPAVED ROADS

Lot developer must construct roadway within platted easement to BMPOA specifications:

  • 3″ compacted #3 gravel base + 2″ crusher run surface
  • 10 ft width between ditches
  • Proper grading/crowning
  • Culverts at low points

Once inspected/accepted by BMPOA Road Committee, maintenance assumed by BMPOA.

ACCESSORY BUILDING REQUIREMENTS

General Requirements

One accessory building per 1-acre lot (other than a garage) if:

  • Complies with Warren County setback/size/height ordinances
  • Matches primary structure in style/materials
  • Pre-approved in writing by ARC

Warren County Definitions

  • Building: Any roofed structure supported by walls/columns
  • Permit Exception: Accessory structures < 200 sq ft in side/rear yard do not require County permit but still need ARC approval
  • Height Limit: 12 ft average height (average of eaves to ridge and wall height)

Accessory Building Setbacks

  • Cannot be placed forward of primary residence's front plane
  • ≥ 50 ft from front property line or any public/private road
  • ≥ 10 ft from side lot lines

Special Provisions

  • Nonconforming Structures: Pre-1992 accessory buildings are grandfathered by County but require ARC pre-approval to avoid covenant violations
  • Foundations & Additions: Accessory buildings (on pilings, skids, slabs) must be in side/rear yard
  • Addition Rule: Within 4 ft of primary structure, considered an addition: requires County permit and must meet primary structure setbacks/height

FENCE REQUIREMENTS

Only split-rail fences allowed. Submit design/materials plan to ARC for written approval. Exceptions case-by-case based on compatibility.

COMPLIANCE AND ENFORCEMENT

Violation Process

  1. Notification: BMPOA/ARC notifies owner in writing of violation and required corrective action
  2. Initial Response: Owner has 60 days to correct or 20 days to submit alternative plan/timetable
  3. Alternative Plan Review: ARC has 30 days to approve/disapprove alternative plan; no response = deemed approved
  4. Final Compliance: If alternative denied, owner has 60 days to comply

Appeals Process

  • Appeals to Arbitration Committee within 30 days
  • If denied, owner must comply within 30 days
  • Board may pursue legal action for continued non-compliance

Final Reminder

ARC approval required even after County approval; violations subject to legal action.

II. A MOUNTAIN HOME

A. COMMUNITY ORIGINS

Scenic mountain vista showcasing the natural beauty that attracted the original developers to Blue Mountain in the late 1950s

The verdant vineyard landscape that characterizes Blue Mountain's agricultural heritage and wine-making tradition

Virginia state flag representing our community's proud heritage in the Commonwealth of Virginia

Created in the late 1950's as a vacation community for Washington D.C. residents, Blue Mountain began as an escape to Virginia's mountain country. The original developers recognized the unique appeal of this location - combining accessible distance from the nation's capital with the tranquil beauty of the Blue Ridge Mountains.

Historical Development

The subdivision was carefully planned to preserve the natural mountain environment while providing modern conveniences for families seeking a retreat from city life. Early development focused on minimal environmental impact and preservation of scenic overlooks.

B. SEASONAL AND PERMANENT RESIDENTS

Blue Mountain has evolved from its origins as a vacation community into a diverse mix of seasonal residents and year-round families. This unique blend creates a dynamic community that honors both its recreational heritage and its role as a permanent home for many families.

Seasonal Residents

  • Washington D.C. area families maintaining vacation homes
  • Multi-generational families with inherited properties
  • Weekend and summer retreat users
  • Retirees with seasonal residency patterns

Year-Round Residents

  • Families who have made Blue Mountain their primary residence
  • Remote workers enjoying mountain lifestyle
  • Retirees seeking peaceful mountain living
  • Local professionals commuting to nearby towns

D. TRILLIUMS

Millions of Great White Trilliums bloom at Thompson Wildlife Management Area each spring

LOCAL ABUNDANCE

Thompson Wildlife Management Area hosts approximately 18 million Great White Trilliums (Trillium grandiflorum), creating one of Virginia's most spectacular spring wildflower displays. Peak bloom occurs in late April to early May each year, drawing nature enthusiasts from across the region to witness this remarkable natural phenomenon.

Peak Viewing

  • Best Time: Late April to early May
  • Location: Thompson Wildlife Management Area
  • Trail Access: Multiple marked trails available
  • Estimated Population: ~18 million blooms

VIEWING AREAS & ACCESS

Trillium Trail Loop

  • Trailhead off Freezeland Road
  • Moderate ~1 mile loop trail
  • Minimal elevation gain
  • Marked path through dense trillium colonies
  • Hardwood canopy provides ideal growing conditions

Appalachian Trail Connector

  • Junction at AT parking area off Route 340/211
  • 2 mile round-trip hike
  • ~700 ft elevation gain to the ridge
  • Extended trillium viewing opportunities

Freezeland Road Access Point

  • Parking pull-off (fits ~3 cars) on Freezeland Road
  • Short unmarked 0.2 mile path into forest
  • Steep and rocky terrain - hiking boots recommended
  • Less crowded viewing area

OTHER NOTABLE FLORA

The same habitat that supports trilliums also nurtures other remarkable spring wildflowers:

  • Lady's Slippers (Cypripedium spp.): Found in wet seeps and boggy areas
  • Showy Orchids (Calopogon tuberosus): Bloom in boggy meadow areas
  • Virginia Bluebells (Mertensia virginica): Line creekbanks in early spring
  • Bloodroot (Sanguinaria canadensis): Pure white flowers emerge before leaves
  • Wild Ginger (Asarum canadense): Hidden burgundy flowers at ground level

Additional scenic mountain vista demonstrating the diverse viewpoints available throughout Blue Mountain

CONSERVATION TIPS

Protect the Resource

  • Do not pick or transplant trilliums - they take ≥9 years to reach flowering maturity
  • Stay on marked trails to avoid trampling the understory
  • Leave no trace: pack out trash, avoid damaging wildflowers
  • Report any illegal picking to Virginia Department of Wildlife Resources

Viewing Ethics

  • Observe and photograph from designated trail areas
  • Keep noise levels low to respect wildlife
  • Visit early morning or late afternoon for best lighting and fewer crowds
  • Respect private property boundaries

E. PLANT RIDGE & VALLEY NATIVES

WHY NATIVE PLANTS MATTER

Native plants are essential for maintaining the ecological integrity of the Ridge & Valley region. They provide critical environmental services that benefit both wildlife and human communities:

Environmental Benefits

  • Stabilize soil and improve water quality by reducing erosion and runoff
  • Provide habitat and food sources for pollinators (bees, butterflies) and wildlife
  • Sequester more CO₂ than non-native species, aiding climate mitigation
  • Enhance biodiversity and ecosystem resilience in the Ridge & Valley region

Practical Advantages

  • Adapted to local climate and soil conditions
  • Require less water, fertilizer, and maintenance
  • More resistant to local pests and diseases
  • Support local ecosystem food webs

RECOMMENDED NATIVE SPECIES

Perennials & Ground Cover

  • Bloodroot (Sanguinaria canadensis): Early spring white flowers
  • Wild Geranium (Geranium maculatum): Pink-purple blooms
  • Trillium (Trillium grandiflorum): Iconic three-petaled white flowers
  • Dutchman's Breeches (Dicentra cucullaria): Unique white flowers
  • Wild Bee Balm (Monarda fistulosa): Lavender pollinator magnet
  • Foam Flower (Tiarella cordifolia): Delicate white flower spikes
  • Wild Ginger (Asarum canadense): Excellent ground cover
  • Milkweed (Asclepias spp.): Essential for monarch butterflies
  • Narrow-Leaf Mountain Mint (Pycnanthemum tenuifolium): Fragrant pollinator plant

Vines

  • Coral Honeysuckle (Lonicera sempervirens): Red tubular flowers
  • Dutchman's Pipe (Aristolochia macrophylla): Large heart-shaped leaves
  • Virgin's Bower (Clematis virginiana): White fragrant flowers
  • Virginia Creeper (Parthenocissus quinquefolia): Excellent fall color

Shrubs

  • Oak Leaf Hydrangea (Hydrangea quercifolia): White cone-shaped flowers
  • Shrubby St. John's Wort (Hypericum prolificum): Yellow flowers
  • Arrowwood Viburnum (Viburnum dentatum): White flowers, blue berries
  • Chokeberry (Aronia arbutifolia): White flowers, red berries
  • Mountain Laurel (Kalmia latifolia): Pink and white clustered blooms

WHERE TO BUY NATIVE PLANTS

Local Nurseries

  • Seven Bends Nursery - Berryville, VA (sevenbends.org)
  • Yellow House Natives - Berryville (via VA Native Plant Society)

Online Resources

  • Virginia Plant Atlas - vaplantatlas.org (distribution maps, seed sources)
  • Plant Virginia Natives - plantvirginianatives.org
  • Download "Plant Ridge & Valley Natives" PDF for detailed guidance

DEER-RESISTANT NATIVE PLANTS

For areas with heavy deer pressure, consider these native species that deer typically avoid:

  • American Holly (Ilex opaca)
  • Black Gum (Nyssa sylvatica)
  • Flowering Dogwood (Cornus florida)
  • Eastern Red Cedar (Juniperus virginiana)

  • Native sedges and grasses

H. HIKING TRAILS ON BLUE MOUNTAIN

Springtime overlook from Blue Mountain showing ridge-top vista with blooming mountain laurel

TRAILHEAD ACCESS & PARKING

Blue Mountain Trailhead

  • GPS Coordinates: 38.9604° N, 78.4703° W
  • Parking: Free gravel lot (~20 spaces)
  • Facilities: Portable restroom onsite
  • Signage: "Blue Mountain Overlook Trail" with map of interconnected loops

Freezeland Road Connector

  • Parking: Small pull-off (fits ~3 vehicles) on gravel road
  • Access: Low clearance vehicles may have difficulty
  • Trail: Unmarked 0.2 mile path through mixed hardwood forest
  • Connects to: Main trail network

Appalachian Trail Junction

  • Location: Official AT parking lot off Route 340/211
  • Distance: Approximately 10 miles north of Blue Mountain
  • Signage: "Blue Mountain Spur" indicates side trail down to BMPOA ridge
  • Facilities: Established parking and trail registration

TRAIL DIFFICULTY & LENGTH

Easy (Beginner)

Deer Run Loop
  • Distance: 1.2 mile loop
  • Elevation Gain: Less than 200 feet
  • Trail Surface: Well-graded footpath, minimal obstacles
  • Features: Interpretive signage, creek crossings, benches
  • Time Required: 45 minutes to 1 hour

Moderate

Blue Ridge Overlook Trail
  • Distance: 3.5 miles out-and-back
  • Elevation Gain: Approximately 800 feet
  • Highlights: Panoramic overlook at ~2,000 feet elevation
  • Features: Rock outcrops, excellent sunrise/sunset views
  • Time Required: 2-3 hours

Strenuous

Appalachian Connector Trail
  • Distance: 5.2 miles one-way to AT summit
  • Elevation Gain: Approximately 1,200 feet
  • Challenges: Steep switchbacks, rocky scrambles, ladder bridges
  • Trailhead: Shared with AT parking area
  • Time Required: 4-6 hours round trip

TRAIL FEATURES & POINTS OF INTEREST

Scenic Overlooks

  • Eagle's View: 0.5 miles from trail start; 180° vista of Shenandoah Valley and ridgelines
  • Sunset Rock: South-facing ledge, popular fall-foliage photography spot
  • Panoramic Point: Highest accessible overlook with views in multiple directions

Flora & Fauna by Season

Spring Ephemerals (Late April–May)
  • Trilliums in dense woodland colonies
  • Virginia bluebells along creek areas
  • Dutchman's breeches on rocky slopes
Summer Berries (July–August)
  • Wild blackberries along Deer Run Loop
  • Native blueberries on sunny ridge areas
  • Elderberries near water sources
Fall Foliage (Mid-October)
  • Sugar maples create brilliant orange displays
  • Oak species provide deep red and brown colors
  • Yellow birches add golden highlights
Wildlife Year-Round
  • Birds: Warblers (Blackburnian, Cerulean), thrushes, occasional bald eagles
  • Mammals: White-tailed deer, occasional black bear sightings, small mammals
  • Safety Note: Exercise caution with wildlife; maintain safe distances

IV. FIRE SAFETY & EMERGENCY PREPAREDNESS

A. WILDFIRE RISK UNDERSTANDING

Proper debris management and fire safety practices are essential for protecting Blue Mountain homes and natural areas

Blue Mountain's beautiful mountain setting also presents wildfire risks that all residents must understand and prepare for. Our community's wooded environment, seasonal drought conditions, and mountain topography create conditions where wildfires can develop and spread rapidly.

Environmental Risk Factors

  • Dense Forest Cover: Mature hardwood and pine forests surround properties
  • Steep Terrain: Mountain slopes accelerate fire spread uphill
  • Seasonal Drought: Summer dry periods increase vegetation flammability
  • Wind Patterns: Mountain winds can rapidly change fire direction
  • Limited Access: Narrow mountain roads may delay emergency response

Human-Caused Fire Risks

  • Power Lines: Electrical equipment can spark fires during high winds
  • Outdoor Burning: Brush piles, campfires, and burn barrels
  • Vehicle Exhaust: Hot exhaust systems in dry grass areas
  • Construction Activity: Equipment use during dry conditions
  • Cigarettes: Improperly discarded smoking materials

Fire Season Awareness

Virginia's fire season typically runs from late February through early June, with peak risk during March and April. However, drought conditions can extend fire danger throughout summer and fall months.

High Risk Periods

  • Late winter/early spring before green-up
  • Extended drought periods
  • High wind events
  • After extended dry spells

Daily Risk Factors

  • Low humidity (below 30%)
  • High temperatures (above 70°F)
  • Wind speeds over 15 mph
  • No recent precipitation

B. EVACUATION ZONES & ROUTES

Blue Mountain's mountain location requires careful evacuation planning. Limited road access means residents must understand evacuation routes and be prepared to leave quickly if authorities order evacuation.

Evacuation Considerations

  • Seasonal Access: Winter conditions may affect evacuation routes
  • Communication: Cell service may be interrupted during emergencies

C. HOME FIRE PROTECTION STRATEGIES

Protecting your home from wildfire requires creating defensible space around structures and using fire-resistant materials and landscaping practices.

Defensible Space Zones

Zone 1: Immediate (0-30 feet from structure)

  • Vegetation: Remove all dead or dying vegetation
  • Trees: Prune branches to 10 feet from roof and other trees
  • Shrubs: Keep well-watered and pruned, away from windows
  • Mulch: Use rock or gravel instead of wood mulch near house
  • Storage: Move firewood and propane tanks 30+ feet away
  • Gutters: Keep clean of leaves and debris

Zone 2: Intermediate (30-100 feet from structure)

  • Fuel Reduction: Thin vegetation to reduce fire intensity
  • Tree Spacing: Maintain 10-foot spacing between tree crowns
  • Understory: Remove ladder fuels that carry fire to tree tops
  • Brush Piles: Remove or relocate away from structures
  • Access: Maintain clear access for emergency vehicles

Zone 3: Extended (100-200 feet from structure)

  • Forest Thinning: Reduce tree density to slow fire spread
  • Fuel Breaks: Create natural fire breaks using roads or clearings
  • Water Sources: Identify and maintain access to water sources
  • Coordination: Work with neighbors on larger fuel reduction projects

Home Hardening Measures

Highest Priority

  • Roof: Metal or tile roofing materials
  • Gutters: Metal gutters with leaf guards
  • Vents: 1/8" mesh screens on all openings
  • Windows: Dual-pane tempered glass
  • Siding: Non-combustible materials when possible

Medium Priority

  • Decks: Fire-resistant decking materials
  • Fencing: Metal or masonry fencing near house
  • Outbuildings: Apply same standards to sheds and garages
  • Driveways: Maintain wide, clear access routes

Strenuous

TRAIL FEATURES & POINTS OF INTEREST

Scenic Overlooks

  • Eagle's View: 0.5 mi from trail start; 180° vista of Shenandoah Valley and ridgelines
  • Sunset Rock: South-facing ledge, popular fall-foliage photo spot

Flora & Fauna

  • Spring Ephemerals: Trilliums, Virginia bluebells, Dutchman's breeches (late April–May)
  • Summer Berries: Blackberries and blueberries (July–Aug) along Deer Run Loop
  • Birdwatching: Warblers (Blackburnian, Cerulean), thrushes, occasional bald eagles
  • Fall Foliage: Peak mid-October; maples, oaks, birches create patchwork of color
  • Wildlife: White-tailed deer, occasional black bear sightings—exercise caution

Historical Points

  • Abandoned Cabin Foundation: ~ 0.8 mi on Blue Ridge Overlook Trail - remnants of mid-1800s settler cabin
  • Old Springhouse Ruins: ~ 1.5 mi on Appalachian Connector Trail - early mountain settlers' spring water collection

Summer

BEAR SAFETY ON THE MOUNTAIN

Understanding bear behavior and prevention measures helps ensure safe coexistence with our mountain wildlife.

UNDERSTANDING BEAR BEHAVIOR

Black bears are intelligent and typically avoid humans; most conflicts occur because they seek out food attractants, not out of aggression.

Common Bear Attractants

  • Bird feeders (April–November)
  • Unsecured garbage cans left outdoors
  • Pet food on porches or yards
  • Grills with residual grease or scraps
  • Fruit trees (ripe or fallen fruit)
  • Compost piles containing meat, bones, or fatty kitchen scraps
  • Coolers, trash, or scented items stored on porches/decks

Preventive Actions for Homeowners

  • Remove bird feeders during active season or switch to bear-resistant models
  • Store garbage in locked garage, shed, or bear-proof container; never leave trash outdoors overnight
  • Feed pets indoors; clean up any food scraps immediately
  • Clean grills after each use (burn off grease for 5 minutes, then cover)
  • Use electric fencing around beehives, compost piles, and gardens
  • Harvest ripe fruit promptly; remove fallen fruit daily
  • Do not store any food or scented items on porches/decks
  • Compost responsibly—no meat, bones, or fatty scraps; use enclosed compost bins if possible

HAZING & DETERRENTS

Safe Hazing Methods

  • Paintballs aimed at the bear's rump (non-lethal, safe deterrent)
  • Loud noises: air horns, shouting, banging pots/pans
  • Motion-activated noise or lighting devices
  • Always maintain an escape route—never corner a bear

IF YOU ENCOUNTER A BEAR

In Your Yard or Home

  • Remain calm—do not run (running can trigger chase response)
  • Identify an exit route; back away slowly while making noise
  • If indoors, open doors to allow bear to exit; do not trap it

While Hiking

  • Hike in groups; make noise around corners and dense vegetation
  • Keep children close and dogs leashed
  • If surprised at close range, speak calmly, back away slowly, and give the bear space
  • Avoid direct eye contact
  • If a bear charges: use bear spray if available; if contact is imminent, fight back using sticks, rocks, or your fists—aim for sensitive areas (nose, eyes)

Bear Behavior Myths Debunked

  • Bears stand on hind legs to see/smell better—not always aggression
  • Black bears' diet is ~80% plant-based (berries, nuts, insects)
  • A mother with cubs usually flees or climbs a tree when threatened; attacks are rare if given space
  • Bears communicate via body language and sounds; they have good color vision and detect movement well
  • Relocating a problem bear often leads another to move into the vacated territory—address attractants instead

Community Cooperation

Discuss bear-safe practices with neighbors; collective effort reduces conflicts.

Important: Feeding bears (intentionally or not) is illegal under Virginia Code (placing food, minerals, or trash to attract bears). Violators face fines and potential legal action.

PLANT RIDGE & VALLEY NATIVES

WHY NATIVE PLANTS MATTER

  • Stabilize soil, improve water quality (reduce erosion/runoff)
  • Provide habitat/food for pollinators (bees, butterflies) and wildlife (birds, small mammals)
  • Sequester more CO₂ than non-native species, aiding climate mitigation
  • Enhance biodiversity and ecosystem resilience in the Ridge & Valley region

RECOMMENDED NATIVE SPECIES

Perennials & Ground Cover

  • Bloodroot (Sanguinaria canadensis)
  • Wild Geranium (Geranium maculatum)
  • Trillium (Trillium grandiflorum)

  • Wild Ginger (Asarum canadense)
  • Milkweed (Asclepias spp.)
  • Narrow-Leaf Mountain Mint (Pycnanthemum tenuifolium)

Vines

  • Virgin's Bower (Clematis virginiana)
